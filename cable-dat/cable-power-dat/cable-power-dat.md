## Nominal Cross-sectional Area


### Understanding Wire Gauge Standards (Renard Series)

The wire sizes you listed (1.5, 2.5, 4, 6, 10, 16 $mm^2$) are standard **Nominal Cross-sectional Areas**. This system is based on the **R'10 priority number series**, which ensures a logical, geometric progression of current-carrying capacity. 

These standards are governed globally by the **IEC (International Electrotechnical Commission)** and in China by **GB/T 5023** and **GB/T 12706**.

---

### Standard Wire Specifications & Typical Applications

| Nominal Area ($mm^2$) | Primary Usage | Recommended Circuit Breaker (MCB) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1.5** | Lighting, low-power control circuits | 10A |
| **2.5** | General wall sockets, small appliances | 16A / 20A |
| **4.0** | Kitchen sockets, water heaters, AC split units | 25A |
| **6.0** | Instant electric water heaters, central AC | 32A / 40A |
| **10.0** | Main power lines (old homes), heavy machinery | 50A / 63A |
| **16.0** | Main residential incoming power lines | 63A / 80A |

---

### Key Regulatory Standards

* **GB/T 3956 (Conductor Resistance):** Defines the electrical resistance limits for conductors. "Non-standard" wires often fail this, having a lower actual cross-section than claimed, which leads to overheating.
* **GB/T 16895 (Low-voltage Electrical Installations):** The master standard for determining **ampacity** (current-carrying capacity) based on environmental factors like ambient temperature, insulation type, and installation method (e.g., in-wall vs. conduit).
* **Insulation Standards:** Specifies thickness and material (e.g., PVC/XLPE) required to maintain dielectric strength and prevent short circuits.

---

### Critical Engineering Considerations

1.  **Thermal Derating:** The values above assume standard installation. If multiple cables are bundled together in the same conduit, heat dissipation is reduced, and you must apply a correction factor (typically multiplying the rated capacity by 0.7 or 0.8).
2.  **Voltage Drop:** For long cable runs (e.g., >30 meters), you must use a larger cross-section than the current requirement suggests to minimize voltage drop, which can cause equipment malfunctions.
3.  **Material Integrity:** These standards are designed for **copper conductors**. If using aluminum, the cross-section must be significantly larger (typically 1.5x) to match the conductivity of copper.



> **Safety Note:** Always verify the "Standard" (GB/T 5023) stamped on the cable jacket. Use only cables that meet national standards to avoid fire hazards caused by undersized or poor-quality conductors.
